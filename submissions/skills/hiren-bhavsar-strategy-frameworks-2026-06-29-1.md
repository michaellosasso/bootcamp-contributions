# Strategy Frameworks

Help the PM choose the right framework for the strategic question, apply it with rigor, and translate findings into a decision or recommendation leadership can act on.

## How to Help

When a PM asks for help with strategy:

1. **Diagnose the question before selecting the framework** — Strategy frameworks are tools, not answers. The first job is to identify what type of strategic question is being asked: Where are we now? Where should we play? How do we win? What should we stop doing? Each question maps to a different set of frameworks. See Framework Selection Guide below.
2. **Apply the framework with intellectual honesty** — A framework applied to confirm a pre-existing conclusion is not strategy; it is post-rationalization. Push for real options, real tradeoffs, and explicit choices. Good strategy requires saying no to things that are good — not just rejecting things that are obviously bad.
3. **Stress-test the strategy** — Apply MECE thinking to the problem structure (no overlaps, no gaps). Ask: what would have to be true for this strategy to fail? Who in the organization is likely to challenge this? Where is the logic weakest?
4. **Translate to a recommendation** — Strategy must end in a choice. Not "we should consider options A, B, and C" but "we recommend A because of X, with these tradeoffs acknowledged." See `references/guest-insights.md` for framework-to-output templates.

## Core Principles

### Strategy is a set of choices, not a plan
Roger Martin (*Playing to Win*): "Strategy is an integrated cascade of choices: a winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, what capabilities are required, and what management systems are needed." A strategy document that does not contain explicit choices — what you will do AND what you will not do — is not a strategy. It is a list of priorities.

### Distinguish good strategy from bad strategy
Richard Rumelt (*Good Strategy / Bad Strategy*): "Bad strategy is not the absence of good strategy. Bad strategy has a life of its own." The hallmarks of bad strategy: fluff (strategy that sounds like strategy but says nothing), failure to face the challenge directly, mistaking goals for strategy ("be number one"), and laundry lists of initiatives with no unifying logic. Good strategy has a kernel: a diagnosis, a guiding policy, and coherent actions.

### Match the framework to the question
A framework applied to the wrong question produces confident-sounding wrong answers. SWOT is a diagnostic, not a strategy. Porter's Five Forces describes industry structure, not your response to it. Ansoff tells you the risk profile of growth options, not which one to choose. Use the Framework Selection Guide to start with the question, then select the tool.

### MECE: structure the problem before solving it
McKinsey principle (via Barbara Minto): "Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive." Before applying any framework, structure the problem space so that categories do not overlap and nothing is left out. A problem structured with MECE logic is much easier to diagnose, prioritize, and communicate. A problem that is not MECE will produce solutions that miss root causes or double-count opportunities.

### Strategy requires explicit tradeoffs — no tradeoff, no strategy
Michael Porter: "The essence of strategy is choosing what NOT to do." A strategy that tries to be all things to all customer segments is not a strategy — it is a failure to choose. The discomfort of saying no is the signal that a real strategic choice is being made. If leadership agrees with everything in the strategy without pushing back, the strategy contains no choices.

### OKRs connect strategy to execution — but only if the O is strategic
John Doerr (*Measure What Matters*): "Ideas are easy. Execution is everything." OKRs work when the Objective is a meaningful strategic bet, not a restatement of business-as-usual. "Improve member engagement" is an aspiration. "Become the first platform members open after a clinical event" is a strategic objective. Key Results must be outcomes (measurable impact) not outputs (tasks delivered).

### Competitive advantage must be defensible
Michael Porter: Competitive advantage comes from either **cost leadership** (deliver the same value at lower cost) or **differentiation** (deliver unique value at a premium price). Trying to do both without explicit tradeoffs leads to being "stuck in the middle" — mediocre at both. Identify which advantage you are building and confirm every strategic initiative reinforces it.

### Use the strategy cascade to connect vision to action
Roger Martin's cascade: **Winning Aspiration → Where to Play → How to Win → Capabilities Required → Management Systems.** Most product strategies break down at the "How to Win" layer — they describe where they will play (market, segment, use case) without specifying the mechanism by which they will beat alternatives. The mechanism is the strategy.

## Questions to Help Users

- "What is the specific strategic question you are trying to answer? (Where are we now? Where should we play? How do we win? What should we stop?)"
- "What decision or recommendation will this strategy work produce? Who is the audience?"
- "What explicit choices does your current strategy contain — what are you saying no to?"
- "Is your problem structure MECE? Can you draw the issue tree?"
- "What would have to be true for your strategy to fail? Have you stress-tested those assumptions?"
- "For your OKRs: are the Objectives strategic bets or restatements of existing work? Are Key Results outcomes or outputs?"
- "What is your source of competitive advantage — cost leadership or differentiation? How does each initiative reinforce it?"
- "Where on the Ansoff matrix is your growth plan? What is the risk profile of each option?"

## Framework Selection Guide

| Strategic Question | Best Framework(s) | Output |
|-------------------|-------------------|--------|
| **Where are we now?** | SWOT, PEST, Competitive Benchmarking, Porter's Five Forces | Situation assessment; strategic gaps |
| **Where should we play?** | Ansoff Matrix, TAM/SAM/SOM sizing, Jobs-to-Be-Done segmentation | Market / segment / use case selection |
| **How do we win?** | Playing to Win (Martin), Value Chain, Core Competencies, Differentiation vs. Cost Leadership | Winning strategy mechanism |
| **What should we prioritize?** | BCG Growth-Share Matrix, ICE/RICE scoring, MoSCoW | Portfolio / initiative prioritization |
| **How do we execute?** | OKRs, Balanced Scorecard, Gap Planning | Goal-setting; execution tracking |
| **What's misaligned internally?** | McKinsey 7S | Org design diagnosis |
| **Is the market attractive?** | Porter's Five Forces, GE-McKinsey Nine-Box | Market entry / investment thesis |
| **How do we structure the problem?** | MECE + issue tree, Hypothesis-driven analysis | Problem structuring before any framework |

## Common Mistakes to Flag

- **Applying SWOT as a strategy** — SWOT is a diagnostic. It tells you where you are. It does not tell you what to do. A SWOT followed by no choices is a completed exercise, not a strategy.
- **Goals masquerading as strategy** — "Become the market leader" is not a strategy. "Win the under-65 high-deductible employer segment by integrating navigation with benefits admin to reduce friction at the moment of care need" is a strategy. Strategy specifies the mechanism of winning, not just the desired outcome.
- **Framework tourism** — Running SWOT, then Porter's, then Ansoff, then a BCG, without a clear decision at the end. Frameworks are inputs to a recommendation, not the deliverable itself.
- **No explicit tradeoffs** — A strategy that all stakeholders immediately agree with contains no real choices. If nothing is being deprioritized or stopped, it is a roadmap with a strategy slide in front of it.
- **OKRs as task lists** — Key Results that are outputs ("launch feature X," "complete project Y") rather than outcomes ("reduce time-to-first-care-event from 14 days to 5 days"). OKRs measure the impact of execution, not the execution itself.
- **Competitive analysis without a "so what"** — A 10-page competitive teardown with no implication for your strategy. Every competitive finding should connect to a strategic choice: this changes what we build, where we play, or how we price.
- **Ignoring the "How to Win" layer** — Most product strategies specify where they will play but not how they will win. "Focus on the employer segment" is a where-to-play. "Win in the employer segment by being the only platform that integrates clinical navigation with real-time benefits eligibility" is a how-to-win.

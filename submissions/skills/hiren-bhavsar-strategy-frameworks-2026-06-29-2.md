## 1. Good Strategy vs. Bad Strategy — Rumelt's Kernel {#good-bad}

**Source:** Richard Rumelt, *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy*

> "Bad strategy is not the absence of good strategy. Bad strategy has a life of its own."

### The hallmarks of bad strategy
1. **Fluff** — Strategy that sounds like strategy but says nothing. "We will leverage our synergistic capabilities to maximize stakeholder value and drive differentiated outcomes." This is not a strategy.
2. **Failure to face the challenge** — Strategy that does not name the hard thing. If your strategy is comfortable and widely agreed upon, it probably does not contain a real diagnosis of the problem.
3. **Goals mistaken for strategy** — "Be the market leader." "Double revenue." "Improve NPS by 20 points." These are outcomes, not strategies. A strategy explains how you will achieve them against specific obstacles.
4. **Laundry lists** — A set of 12 "strategic initiatives" with no unifying logic is not a strategy. It is a budget allocation dressed up as a strategic plan.

### The kernel of good strategy
Every good strategy has three parts:

| Part | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Diagnosis** | Names the challenge clearly — the specific obstacle or opportunity that must be addressed | "Our cost-per-engagement is 3x the industry average because our activation funnel has no moment of perceived value in the first 7 days" |
| **Guiding Policy** | The approach for dealing with the challenge — what you will and will not do | "Win through clinical relevance at the moment of need, not broad platform marketing" |
| **Coherent Actions** | Specific, coordinated moves that reinforce the guiding policy | "Build event-triggered nudge pipeline; redesign onboarding around first care event; deprioritize general wellness content" |

**The test:** Can you state the diagnosis in one sentence? If not, the strategy is not clear enough to execute.

## 2. Framework: Playing to Win — Roger Martin's Cascade {#playing-to-win}

**Source:** Roger Martin & A.G. Lafley, *Playing to Win: How Strategy Really Works*

> "Strategy is an integrated cascade of choices: a winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, what capabilities are required, and what management systems are needed."

### The five choices

**1. Winning Aspiration**
The purpose of the enterprise. Not a financial target — a description of what winning looks like from the customer's perspective.
- Not: "Be the top-rated health navigation platform"
- Yes: "Be the platform every member turns to at the moment they face a health decision"

**2. Where to Play**
The specific arenas in which you will compete. Must be specific — not "the health benefits space" but a combination of:
- Which customer segments
- Which geographies or verticals
- Which channels
- Which product categories or use cases

"Where to play" is a choice to NOT play everywhere else. If you have not excluded something, you have not made a choice.

**3. How to Win**
The mechanism by which you beat alternatives in the chosen arenas. The most neglected layer of most product strategies.
- Not: "Provide better care navigation"
- Yes: "Win through integrated real-time benefits eligibility so members never hit a dead end; and through advocate-guided care so members with complex needs always have a human escalation path"

The "How to Win" must be defensible — hard for competitors to replicate.

**4. Capabilities Required**
The specific activities and competencies that must be in place to execute the How to Win.
- Example: Real-time data integrations, clinical content taxonomy, advocate workflow tooling, NLP-based triage

**5. Management Systems**
The metrics, incentives, and processes that reinforce the strategy.
- Example: PMPM per employer, activation rate by cohort, advocate utilization rate

### The cascade test
Each choice must reinforce the others. Ask for every initiative: Does this support our How to Win? Does our How to Win require this capability? Does the management system measure whether this is working?

If an initiative cannot be justified by tracing it through the cascade, it does not belong in the strategy.

## 3. Framework: MECE — Structure Before You Solve {#mece}

**Source:** McKinsey principle; Barbara Minto (*The Pyramid Principle*); Paul Millerd (*StrategyU*)

> "MECE thinking separates good problem-solvers from great ones."

**MECE = Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive**
- **Mutually Exclusive:** No overlap between categories. If a problem piece belongs in two buckets, the buckets are wrong.
- **Collectively Exhaustive:** All possibilities are covered. If you can think of a scenario that does not fit any bucket, the structure is incomplete.

### The issue tree
Apply MECE by building an issue tree before choosing any framework:
1. State the central question in one sentence
2. Break it into 3–5 non-overlapping sub-questions
3. For each sub-question, ask: is there any scenario that belongs here AND in another branch? Is there any scenario that belongs nowhere?
4. Repeat until you reach a level where the answers are findable

**Example — "Why is member activation below target?"**
```
Why is activation below target?
├── Awareness gap (members don't know the platform exists)
│   ├── Employer communication reach
│   └── Benefits enrollment touchpoint coverage
├── Motivation gap (members know but don't see value)
│   ├── Value proposition relevance by segment
│   └── First-session experience quality
└── Friction gap (members want to but can't)
    ├── Registration completion rate
    └── App availability / device compatibility
```

Each branch is mutually exclusive. Together they are collectively exhaustive (all possible causes of low activation fit somewhere). This structure tells you exactly where to look for the root cause before choosing any solution.

### MECE in communication
MECE presentations are more persuasive because:
- The audience never wonders "where does this fit?" or "what am I missing?"
- Arguments do not contradict each other
- It is clear when the argument is complete

## 4. Framework: SWOT Analysis {#swot}

**Source:** Widely attributed; standardized in the 1960s–70s

**Use when:** Diagnosing the current state at the start of a strategic planning cycle.

**What it is NOT:** A strategy. SWOT tells you where you are. It does not tell you what to do.

| Category | Definition | PM application |
|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Strengths** | Internal factors where the organization has an advantage | Clinical data depth, advocate network, employer relationships, platform integrations |
| **Weaknesses** | Internal factors that put the organization at a disadvantage | Activation friction, product-market fit gaps, technical debt, low brand awareness |
| **Opportunities** | External factors that could be exploited | Employer benefits consolidation trend, AI-enabled personalization, new regulatory tailwinds |
| **Threats** | External factors that could harm the organization | Competing navigation platforms, employer cost pressure reducing benefits spend, data privacy regulation |

### From SWOT to strategy
SWOT alone produces a list. To convert it to a strategy, apply the SO/WO/ST/WT matrix:
- **SO (Strengths + Opportunities):** How can you use your strengths to capture this opportunity?
- **WO (Weaknesses + Opportunities):** How can you fix a weakness to capture this opportunity?
- **ST (Strengths + Threats):** How can you use your strengths to neutralize this threat?
- **WT (Weaknesses + Threats):** How can you minimize a weakness to avoid this threat?

The SO quadrant typically produces your growth strategy. The WT quadrant surfaces your most urgent defensive moves.

## 5. Framework: Porter's Five Forces {#porters}

**Source:** Michael E. Porter, *Competitive Strategy* (1980), Harvard Business School

**Use when:** Assessing industry attractiveness before entering a market or making a major investment.

> "Competition isn't just about who has the best product at the best price. Using Porter's Five Forces forces you to take a step back."

| Force | Question | High = Bad for profitability |
|-------|---------|------------------------------|
| **Rivalry among existing competitors** | How intense is competition among current players? | Many competitors, low differentiation, high fixed costs |
| **Threat of new entrants** | How easy is it for new competitors to enter? | Low barriers to entry, no switching costs, no scale advantage |
| **Bargaining power of buyers** | How much leverage do customers have? | Concentrated buyers, low switching costs, price sensitivity |
| **Bargaining power of suppliers** | How much leverage do input providers have? | Few suppliers, high switching costs, critical inputs |
| **Threat of substitutes** | Can customers solve their need a different way? | Many alternatives, low switching cost, improving quality of substitutes |

### Application process
1. Define the industry precisely (too broad = meaningless analysis)
2. Rate each force: high / medium / low pressure on industry profitability
3. Identify which 1–2 forces are dominant
4. Draw strategic implications: if buyer power is high, where is the defensible differentiation? If substitutes are the primary threat, what switches the customer's calculus?

## 6. Framework: Ansoff Growth Matrix {#ansoff}

**Source:** Igor Ansoff, *Corporate Strategy* (1965)

**Use when:** Evaluating growth options and their associated risk.

|  | **Existing Markets** | **New Markets** |
|--|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Existing Products** | **Market Penetration** — Lowest risk. Sell more of what you have to who already buys it. | **Market Development** — Moderate risk. Same product, new segment, geography, or channel. |
| **New Products** | **Product Development** — Moderate risk. New product for existing customers. Leverages existing relationships. | **Diversification** — Highest risk. New product, new market. Requires new capabilities and new customer knowledge. |

### Risk profiles
- **Market Penetration:** Increase activation, improve retention, upsell to current employer base. Lowest risk — you understand the customer and the product.
- **Product Development:** Add a new capability (e.g., scheduling, benefits admin integration) for existing members. Risk is execution and adoption.
- **Market Development:** Take the existing platform to a new employer type, geography, or distribution partner. Risk is product-market fit in the new context.
- **Diversification:** Enter a new vertical with a new offering. Highest risk — treat as a new company within the company.

**Strategic implication:** Most organizations underinvest in market penetration and over-index on new products. Before building new, ask: have you maximized what you have with the customers who already trust you?

## 7. Framework: BCG Growth-Share Matrix {#bcg}

**Source:** Boston Consulting Group, 1970; Bruce Henderson

**Use when:** Prioritizing investment across a product portfolio or set of business units.

|  | **High Market Share** | **Low Market Share** |
|--|----------------------|---------------------|
| **High Growth** | ⭐ **Stars** — Invest heavily. High share in a growing market. Tomorrow's cash cows. | ❓ **Question Marks** — Selective investment. High growth but unproven share. Evaluate potential before committing. |
| **Low Growth** | 🐄 **Cash Cows** — Harvest. Generate cash to fund Stars and Question Marks. Protect, don't over-invest. | 🐕 **Dogs** — Divest or minimize. Low share, low growth. Consume resources without building position. |

### PM application
Use this framework to pressure-test portfolio balance:
- Do you have enough Cash Cows funding your Stars?
- Are you over-investing in Dogs (features or products with neither growth nor share)?
- Are your Question Marks receiving structured evaluation (time-bounded experiments with clear thresholds) or indefinite investment?

**Common trap:** Treating everything as a Star and under-harvesting the Cash Cows that fund growth.

## 8. Framework: McKinsey 7S {#7s}

**Source:** McKinsey & Company; Tom Peters & Robert Waterman, *In Search of Excellence* (1982)

**Use when:** Diagnosing why a strategy is not being executed, or why an organizational change is failing.

The 7S framework recognizes that organizational effectiveness requires alignment across seven interdependent elements. Change one, and you affect them all.

| Element | Definition | Diagnostic question |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Strategy** | The plan for competitive advantage | Does our strategy specify choices and tradeoffs? |
| **Structure** | How the organization is organized (hierarchy, reporting) | Does structure enable or impede the strategy? |
| **Systems** | Processes, workflows, and decision-making procedures | Do our systems reinforce strategic priorities? |
| **Shared Values** | The culture and core beliefs | Does culture reward the behaviors the strategy requires? |
| **Style** | Leadership approach and management culture | Does how we lead model what the strategy needs? |
| **Staff** | The people — skills, talent, roles | Do we have the right people in the right roles? |
| **Skills** | Core competencies of the organization | Do organizational capabilities match strategic requirements? |

**The hardest alignment:** Strategy ↔ Shared Values. You can change structure quickly. You cannot change culture quickly. If the strategy requires bold risk-taking but the culture is risk-averse, the strategy will fail regardless of how well-designed it is.

## 9. Framework: OKRs — Objectives and Key Results {#okrs}

**Source:** Andy Grove (Intel); popularized by John Doerr, *Measure What Matters*; adopted at Google, Spotify, Airbnb

**Use when:** Translating strategy into measurable quarterly goals at the team or product level.

> "Ideas are easy. Execution is everything." — John Doerr

### The OKR structure

**Objective:** A qualitative, inspiring, time-bound goal. It should answer: "What do we want to achieve?" It must be a real strategic bet, not a restatement of business-as-usual.

| Weak Objective | Strong Objective |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Improve member engagement | Become the first platform members open when they face a health decision |
| Launch the scheduling feature | Make care access effortless for members with complex needs |
| Hit Q3 activation target | Double activation in the under-65 high-deductible segment |

**Key Results:** 3–5 measurable outcomes that define success for the Objective. Must be outcomes, not outputs.

| Output (wrong) | Outcome (right) |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Launch push notification campaign | Activation rate in target cohort rises from 34% to 52% |
| Complete product discovery for scheduling | 30% of members who navigate to a specialist complete a booking |
| Deliver 3 new SDOH data integrations | Risk stratification accuracy improves by 15% (validated by clinical team) |

### OKR quality test
For each Key Result, ask:
1. Is it measurable? (Can you put a number on it?)
2. Is it an outcome? (Does it measure impact, not just delivery?)
3. Is it ambitious but achievable? (60–70% attainment rate is healthy — OKRs are not performance reviews)
4. Is it owned? (One person is accountable for the number)

### Common OKR failure modes
- **Sandbagging:** Setting KRs you know you can hit. OKRs should require real effort and carry real risk of missing.
- **Output KRs:** "Ship X feature" is a task. "Reduce time-to-first-care-event from 14 days to 5" is a Key Result.
- **Too many OKRs:** 3–5 Key Results per Objective. More than that signals lack of prioritization.
- **OKRs without strategy:** OKRs that do not connect to a strategic objective are just project management.

## 10. Framework: Balanced Scorecard {#bsc}

**Source:** Robert Kaplan & David Norton, Harvard Business Review (1992)

**Use when:** Tracking strategy execution across multiple dimensions — not just financial performance.

The Balanced Scorecard measures four perspectives to prevent short-term financial focus from hiding long-term strategic erosion.

| Perspective | Question | Example Metrics |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| **Financial** | How do we look to shareholders / leadership? | Revenue, PMPM savings, margin, cost to serve |
| **Customer** | How do customers perceive us? | NPS, member activation, retention, engagement depth |
| **Internal Processes** | What must we excel at? | Time-to-activation, care navigation completion rate, advocate utilization |
| **Learning & Growth** | Can we continue to improve and innovate? | Trait development velocity, model accuracy improvement, team capability scores |

**Scorecard → Initiatives → Action items:** Each perspective generates Objectives → Measures → Initiatives → specific Action Items owned by a named person.

**PM application:** Use the Balanced Scorecard to audit whether your roadmap is balanced across all four perspectives, or over-indexed on one (typically internal processes / features) at the expense of others (learning, customer, financial impact).

## 11. Framework: Gap Planning {#gap}

**Source:** Widely used in strategic planning; also called "needs assessment"

**Use when:** Bridging the gap between current state and the vision.

**Structure:**
1. **Vision:** What does the ideal state look like?
2. **Current State:** What is the honest assessment of where you are today?
3. **Gap:** What specific obstacles, deficiencies, or missing capabilities explain the distance?
4. **Improvement Plan:** What specific changes close which parts of the gap?

**Example:**

| Layer | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Vision | Every at-risk member receives a personalized outreach within 48 hours of a clinical trigger |
| Current State | Outreach is batch/weekly; median time-to-outreach is 9 days; no real-time trigger pipeline |
| Gap | No event-driven messaging infrastructure; advocate routing is manual; member risk scores recalculate weekly not daily |
| Improvement | Build event-triggered nudge pipeline (Q2); automate advocate routing by risk tier (Q3); implement daily risk recalculation (Q4) |

**Gap planning is powerful in executive presentations** because it creates a clear before/after narrative without requiring the audience to infer the problem from a list of initiatives.

## 12. Framework: Business Model Canvas {#bmc}

**Source:** Alexander Osterwalder & Yves Pigneur, *Business Model Generation* (2010)

**Use when:** Designing or stress-testing a business model; evaluating a new product or partnership.

Nine building blocks on one page:

| Block | Question |
|-------|---------|
| **Value Propositions** | What unique value do we deliver? What problem do we solve? |
| **Customer Segments** | Who are we creating value for? Who are our most important customers? |
| **Channels** | How do we reach customers? How do we deliver value? |
| **Customer Relationships** | What relationship does each segment expect us to establish? |
| **Revenue Streams** | How do we earn revenue from each segment? What are they willing to pay? |
| **Key Resources** | What assets does the value proposition require? |
| **Key Activities** | What critical activities does our value proposition require? |
| **Key Partners** | Who are our key suppliers and partners? What do we outsource? |
| **Cost Structure** | What are the most important costs? Which key resources and activities are most expensive? |

**PM application:** Use the Business Model Canvas to quickly identify whether a new product initiative requires new key resources or key activities that the organization does not currently have — before committing to build.

## 13. Framework: PEST Analysis {#pest}

**Source:** Widely attributed; popularized in strategic planning literature

**Use when:** Understanding the external macro-environment before building strategy.

| Factor | What it covers | Healthcare example |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Political** | Government regulations, policy, legal factors, tax guidelines | CMS value-based care rules, ACA provisions, employer mandate changes |
| **Economic** | Inflation, interest rates, employment, purchasing power | Employer cost pressure on benefits spend, risk of benefits cuts in recession |
| **Sociocultural** | Demographics, lifestyle, consumer behavior, health literacy | Aging workforce, rising chronic disease prevalence, digital health adoption by age cohort |
| **Technological** | Innovation, R&D pace, automation, digital adoption | AI triage, wearables, EHR interoperability, telehealth regulatory evolution |

**Extended: PESTLE** adds **Legal** (contracts, IP, employment law) and **Environmental** (sustainability, climate health impacts).

**Output:** A PEST is not a strategy — it is a context-setting exercise. Every finding should connect to an opportunity or threat in your SWOT, and from there to a strategic choice.

## 14. Framework: GE-McKinsey Nine-Box Matrix {#nine-box}

**Source:** McKinsey & Company, developed for General Electric in the 1970s

**Use when:** Prioritizing investment across business units or product lines with two dimensions: industry attractiveness and competitive strength.

|  | **Strong Competitive Position** | **Medium** | **Weak Competitive Position** |
|--|--------------------------------|------------|-------------------------------|
| **High Industry Attractiveness** | Invest / Grow | Selective investment | Selective investment |
| **Medium** | Selective investment | Selective investment / Harvest | Harvest / Divest |
| **Low Industry Attractiveness** | Harvest | Harvest | Divest |

More nuanced than the BCG matrix because it uses multiple factors (not just market share and growth rate) to score each axis.

**Industry Attractiveness factors:** Market size, growth rate, profitability, competitive intensity, technology requirements, environmental factors.

**Competitive Strength factors:** Market share, brand strength, production capacity, profit margins relative to competitors, technology level, customer loyalty.

## 15. Framework: Value Chain Analysis {#value-chain}

**Source:** Michael Porter, *Competitive Advantage* (1985)

**Use when:** Identifying where a company creates value, where costs are concentrated, and where differentiation can be built.

**Primary Activities** (directly create and deliver value):
1. Inbound logistics — receiving and handling inputs
2. Operations — transforming inputs to outputs
3. Outbound logistics — delivery to customer
4. Marketing and sales — creating demand
5. Service — after-sale support

**Support Activities** (enable primary activities):
- Infrastructure — management systems
- Human resources — talent acquisition and development
- Technology development — R&D, tooling, data
- Procurement — sourcing

**PM application:** Map your product against the value chain to identify:
- Where differentiation is possible (where competitors do it the same way and you can do it better)
- Where costs are concentrated (and whether cost leadership is achievable)
- Where integration with a partner adds more value than building independently

## 16. Framework Selection Guide — Question to Tool {#selection-guide}

Use this table to match the strategic question to the right framework.

| Strategic Question | Primary Framework | Supporting Frameworks |
|-------------------|------------------|-----------------------|
| Where are we now? What are our strengths and gaps? | SWOT | PEST, Benchmarking, Competitive analysis |
| How attractive is this market / industry? | Porter's Five Forces | GE-McKinsey Nine-Box, TAM/SAM/SOM |
| Where should we compete? | Ansoff Matrix, Playing to Win (Where to Play) | BCG Matrix, Customer segmentation |
| How do we win against alternatives? | Playing to Win (How to Win), Value Chain, Core Competencies | Porter's differentiation vs. cost leadership |
| How do we structure this problem? | MECE issue tree | Hypothesis-driven analysis |
| How do we prioritize our portfolio? | BCG Growth-Share Matrix | GE-McKinsey, ICE/RICE scoring |
| How do we connect strategy to execution? | OKRs, Balanced Scorecard | Gap Planning |
| Why isn't our strategy being executed? | McKinsey 7S | SWOT (internal factors) |
| Is our business model sound? | Business Model Canvas | Value Chain |
| What external forces should we plan for? | PEST / PESTLE | Porter's Five Forces |

**The meta-rule:** Always structure the problem with MECE before choosing any framework. A well-structured problem often suggests its own framework. A poorly structured problem will produce misleading output regardless of which framework you apply.
